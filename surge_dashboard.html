Weedy seadragon habitat · wave exposure

Does the seabed at Kurnell get shaken as hard as at Avalon?

Two weedy-seadragon populations, one sheltered and shallow, one exposed and deep. This compares the near-bed surge — the back-and-forth water motion at the animals' depth — across all of 2025. The finding: Kurnell's everyday surge is slightly stronger and far steadier, while Avalon's bed takes the bigger storm hits.

What went in

Inputs & method

Everything below is built from one year of modelled wave conditions at each site, converted to seabed motion with standard wave physics. No field measurements of surge were used.

How surge is computed. Near-bed orbital velocity from linear (Airy) wave theory: u_b = π·Hs / (T·sinh kh), where h is the specimen depth and k the wavenumber. Wave height is first shoaled from each point's contour down to the specimen depth (Kurnell 10→12 m, factor ×0.983; Avalon 30→20 m, factor ×0.986), with peak period as the representative period. The deep-water sinh kh term is what strips motion out before it reaches a deeper bed — the heart of the whole comparison.

The four views

Reading the graphs

Each chart looks at the same two year-long surge records from a different angle — how often, how spread out, when in the year, and why depth matters.

1

Exceedance

How often the bed is moving faster than a given speed. Pick a speed on the bottom axis; the curve tells you what share of the year the surge is above it. Kurnell sits higher through the moderate range — its bed is busy more of the time — but the curves cross near the top, where Avalon's bigger storms pull ahead.
2

Distribution

The full spread of hourly surge speeds, with dashed lines at each median. Kurnell's hump sits a little to the right — a stronger typical surge — while Avalon's longer right-hand tail is its heavier storm hours reaching down to the deeper bed.
3

Seasonal

Month-by-month surge: solid = median, dotted = 95th percentile (storms). The median gap holds all year (Kurnell steadily on top), but the storm lines trade places in the rougher months, when Avalon's long swell reaches its bed.
4

Why depth matters

Each dot is one hour: wave height offshore (across) against surge at the seabed (up). For the same wave height, Avalon's dots sit lower — the deeper bed feels less of it. Avalon only catches up when its waves are genuinely larger. This is the depth penalty in one picture.

The one-line takeaway

Despite Avalon's much bigger waves, eight extra metres of water column mean the two seadragon beds feel a similar everyday surge — Kurnell's is marginally stronger and much steadier — while Avalon takes the heavier storm peaks. Sheltered-and-shallow is not the calmer bed it sounds like.

Data: NSW Nearshore Wave Tool hindcast, 2025 — Kurnell point #103358 (specimen depth 12 m) and Avalon point #303001 (specimen depth 20 m). Method: near-bed orbital velocity, linear wave theory, wave height shoaled to specimen depth, peak period as representative period.
  • Shoaling only — refraction is neglected (the shoaling correction here is under 2%, so this is a minor term).
  • Linear (Airy) theory with a single representative period; fine for a site-to-site comparison, not a substitute for a full spectral bottom-stress model.
  • The 0.2 / 0.4 m/s reference levels are illustrative — swap in a species-specific threshold if you have one.
  • Specimen depths are population averages; the everyday-surge ranking is sensitive to roughly ±1 m at the shallow Kurnell site.
